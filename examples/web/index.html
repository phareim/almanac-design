— No. XII · Spring

The Almanac

— from the editor

The almanac is a quiet object. It does not flash; it does not push. It opens to where the reader left off and waits. This page is a practice run for that posture — a few words, a few rules, no chrome.

— I. The Page

A measure that lets the eye return

Sixty-five characters per line is not a fashion. It is the width at which the eye, having reached the right edge, can find the next line without searching. Wider, and the reader slows; narrower, and the rhythm of paragraph and sentence breaks down.

Type sets in Source Serif 4 at fourteen pixels with a line-height of one-and-a-half. The serif slows the eye to a reading pace, which is the point.

— II. The Mark

The orbital glyph is the only logo. A circle, an ellipse off-axis, a single accent dot. It is sized at thirty-eight units and may bleed past its reserved bounds, the way a planetary orbit does past the page that tries to contain it.

— III. The Conversation

Chat is a serif column. There are no bubbles; bubbles are a chrome invented for sales. The almanac form is the dialogue:

— YOU

Why so few stars?

— AGENT

Because each one has to earn its place. Six hand-placed marks evoke a sky; three hundred drifting particles evoke a screensaver.
